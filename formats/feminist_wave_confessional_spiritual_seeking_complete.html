CHAPTER 1

The first time I saw God, I was nine years old and trying to shave my legs with my mother’s Lady Bic in a bathroom that smelled of mildew and Jean Naté. The tub was avocado green, a great chipped beast of a thing, and I had one foot propped on its cold lip, my knee a sharp, bony island. The radio on the wicker hamper was tuned to the oldies station, Diana Ross pleading Ain’t No Mountain High Enough through a haze of static. I wasn’t supposed to be doing this. My legs, according to my mother, were still the legs of a child, covered in fine, invisible blonde down. But I had seen the older girls at the pool, their limbs slick and bare as seals, and I understood, with a clarity that felt like a stone in my gut, that hairlessness was not a state of being but a project. A becoming.

I sawed at my shin, dry, no cream, the plastic razor tugging and catching. A red bead welled up, a perfect, shocking ruby. I watched it gather, a tiny planet of pain. And then, a shift. Not a sound, not a light. A pressure change, as if the bathroom itself took a deep, silent breath. The air went thick, honeyed. The mildew smell vanished, replaced by something clean and vast, like ozone after lightning, or the inside of a glacier. Diana Ross didn’t stop singing, but her voice became the only sound in a universe that had suddenly focused to a single, humming point: me, the blood, the avocado tub.

In the steam-streaked mirror, over my own small, frowning shoulder, a presence coalesced. Not a face, not a figure. A quality. An immense, tender attention. It had no gender, but it felt maternal in the way the deep ocean is maternal—encompassing, ancient, utterly indifferent to your small fears. It regarded my bleeding shin, my clumsy project of transformation, and I felt a wave of… amusement? Not mockery. A cosmic, gentle chuckle. The feeling poured into me, not as words, but as a direct knowing: You are trying so hard to carve a woman out of this girl. The blueprint is already written in your bones. The mountain you are climbing is your own skin.

Then, as suddenly as it came, it was gone. The air thinned. Jean Naté and mildew rushed back into the vacuum. Diana Ross hit the chorus, triumphant. I was just a girl in a bathroom with a nicked shin, holding a pink razor.

I never told anyone. Not my mother, who was in the kitchen making meatloaf, her own womanhood a tired uniform of polyester pants and sighing resentment. Not my best friend, Leah, who was still happily hairy, collecting tadpoles in jars. What would I say? God watched me shave and thought it was cute? It sounded insane. It felt, instead, like a secret pact. A private orientation. I had been seen, not by the scowling, bearded God of Sunday school flannelgraphs, but by something vast and intimate that found my fumbling femininity worthy of a visit.

That was my first spiritual experience. It didn’t happen in a church. It happened in a bathroom, with a bleeding leg and disco on the radio. It set the pattern.

My mother’s feminism was a quiet, exhausted war fought on the terrain of casseroles and credit card bills. She had a single, dog-eared copy of The Feminine Mystique on her nightstand, buried under issues of Good Housekeeping. Her rebellion was in the details: she kept her maiden name as a middle initial—L. for Lawson—on the checking account. She sometimes drank a glass of Chablis while making dinner, a small act of secular communion. She had wanted to be a librarian, but married my father, a kind, baffled man who sold insurance and thought “women’s lib” meant she got to choose the color of the new sedan.

Her guidance on womanhood was a series of practical, grim prophecies. “Always have your own money, Maggie. It’s the only voice they really hear.” And, “Your body will be a battlefield. Learn the topography early.” She said this while teaching me to insert a tampon, the two of us locked in that same avocado bathroom, the applicator diagram on the box looking like a cartoon rocket ship. Her hands were steady, but her voice was flat, a news anchor reporting from a war zone.

I wanted a different map. I scoured the teen magazines, their pages glossy with promises. Ten Ways to Make Him Notice! Kissable Lips! Your Dream Life Awaits! The project continued. I learned to curl my lashes, to apply foundation in downward strokes, to smile without showing my gums. I practiced in the mirror, trying on expressions like hats: mysterious, approachable, intelligent-but-not-intimidating. It felt like preparing for a play where I didn’t know the lines, but everyone in the audience held a script.

At sixteen, I fell in love with a boy named Jason who played bass in a band that covered Nirvana badly. His attention was a spotlight. I molded myself into the shape of its beam: the girl who liked his music, who thought his angst was profound, who didn’t mind the smell of clove cigarettes and unwashed flannel. We had sex for the first time in his parents’ paneled basement, on a plaid couch that smelled of wet dog and potato chips. It was awkward, fumbling, a little painful. As I stared at the wood-paneled ceiling, a water stain shaped like Australia, I felt that presence again. Not the vast, tender attention of the bathroom, but something closer, a whisper in the marrow. This is also a ritual. This, too, is a becoming. It didn’t make the act sacred, but it made it mine. A data point. An experience I was having, not just a service I was performing.

Afterwards, Jason rolled over and said, “You’re not like other girls.” He meant it as the highest compliment. I felt a fracture open inside me, a tiny, clean crack. Which girls wasn’t I like? My mother? Leah? The glossy, kissable-lipped girls in the magazines? Who were “other girls,” and why was not being them the goal? The presence within me hummed, a low, agreeing note. The crack widened. A sliver of light got in.

I went to college on a scholarship and a pile of loans, a secular convent where the gospel was Critical Theory and the incense was patchouli. I swapped Seventeen for Simone de Beauvoir, lip gloss for a raised fist. In Women’s Studies 101, a professor with magnificent silver hair and a tapestry vest told us, “The personal is political.” She said it like a spell, and it was. It named the fracture. My mother’s tired war, the bathroom project, Jason’s backhanded compliment—they weren’t just my story. They were the story. I felt righteous, purified. I stopped shaving my legs. I wore big boots. I learned to say “patriarchy” like it was a curse that could break bones.

My spiritual life went underground. The God of the avocado bathroom didn’t fit in the seminar room. That God felt too soft, too personal, too accepting of the very categories we were trying to smash. My seeking became intellectual, furious, and entirely external. I sought answers in books, in protests, in the brilliant, angry women around me. We were going to think our way out of the cage.

I fell in love again, with a woman named Sam. She had dark eyes that didn’t miss a thing and a laugh that sounded like breaking glass. She was a poetry major who could take apart a sexist line of verse or a carburetor with equal, cool precision. Our love felt like a revolutionary act. A claiming. In her narrow dorm bed, tangled in sheets covered in anarchist symbols, I felt powerful, seen for my mind, my politics, my self. It was a different kind of becoming.

One night, after a rally, we lay spent, the street chants still echoing in our blood. The room was dark, lit only by the red tip of her cigarette. “Do you ever feel,” I whispered, the words leaving me before I could cage them, “like there’s something else? Something… underneath all the theory?”

She took a long drag. The ember flared, illuminating the sharp, beautiful plane of her cheek. “You mean like collective consciousness? Historical materialist force?”

“No. I mean… like a presence. A kind of… knowing.”

She was silent for a long time. She stubbed out the cigarette. “That’s the opiate, Maggie. That’s the ghost they put in the machine to keep us from seeing the gears. Feelings are traps. Structure is real.”

I felt the avocado God, that vast tenderness, retreat. A door I hadn’t known was ajar clicked shut. I kissed her shoulder, tasting salt and smoke. “You’re right,” I said. “Of course you’re right.”

But I was lying. To her, and to myself. The ghost in my machine had a heartbeat. And it was mine.

After graduation, the world demanded a shape. Sam went to law school to fight the gears from within. I moved to the city, got a job at a non-profit that advocated for girls’ education. My feminism became my career, a tidy, grant-funded box. I lived in a walk-up apartment with a fire escape that bloomed with geraniums in cheap pots. I dated a kind, earnest man who worked in renewable energy. We drank fair-trade coffee and had respectful, negotiated sex. I was a modern woman. I had the politics, the partner, the purpose. And I was quietly, persistently, dying of thirst.

The presence didn’t come in waves anymore. It was a dull, persistent ache. A homesickness for a home I’d never seen. I tried to fill it. I did yoga in mirrored studios where the instructor urged us to “set an intention.” I went to a feminist meditation group where we deconstructed the mindfulness-industrial complex. I read eco-spiritual manifestos and rolled my eyes at their essentialism. Nothing fit. I was a jigsaw piece from three different puzzles.

The crack that had started with Jason’s comment was now a canyon. On one side: the intellectual, political, rigorous self I had built. On the other: the girl in the bathroom who knew, in her bones, that she had been touched by something sacred. They shouted across the void, each calling the other a traitor.

One Tuesday, after a soul-grinding day of budget meetings, I found myself not on the train home, but pushing open the heavy oak door of a church. Not my childhood church. This was an old, high-ceilinged thing downtown, smelling of stone and old wood and cold wax. It was empty. No services, no music. Just the silent, waiting space.

I sat in a back pew. I didn’t pray. I didn’t know how. I just sat, letting the weight of my own contradictions press me into the hard wood. The stained-glass windows glowed with the dying afternoon light, throwing jeweled shadows on the floor. I thought of my mother’s tired war, of Sam’s brilliant certainty, of the Lady Bic and the blood.

And then, a familiar shift. The honeyed air. The vast, quiet attention. It didn’t feel like coming from the altar, from the cross. It felt like it was rising from the stone floor itself, from the very dust in the shafts of light. It held my fractured self—the theorist, the seeker, the girl, the woman—in one, seamless gaze.

No words this time. Just a feeling, immense and gentle as a tide: Welcome back to the project.

Tears came. Hot, silent, embarrassing. They weren’t tears of sadness or joy, but of recognition. Like finally hearing your name called after a long time in a noisy crowd.

I didn’t find God that day. I found the thread. The one that started in an avocado bathroom. The one I’d been dropping, picking up, and tripping over my whole life. I walked out into the city twilight, the streetlights buzzing to life. My phone buzzed in my pocket—a text from my boyfriend about dinner. The world snapped back into its sharp, demanding focus.

But something was different. The canyon was still there. But I was no longer on one side, shouting. I was the canyon itself. And deep in its dark, quiet heart, something was beginning, terribly, to grow.

CHAPTER 2

“You’re doing what?”

Leah’s voice, sharp with disbelief, traveled cleanly through the phone line from Portland, where she was a tenured professor of marine biology and the mother of two boys who, in the photos she sent, were always covered in what looked like alien mud.

“A silent retreat,” I repeated, stirring a pot of lentil soup on my apartment’s two-burner stove. “For a weekend. It’s not a cult. It’s in the Catskills.”

“A silent retreat,” she said again, as if translating from a dubious language. “Maggie, you hate silence. You narrate your own grocery shopping. You talked through the entirety of Schindler’s List.”

“That was in college! And I was anxious.”

“You’re always anxious. That’s your brand. What’s this really about? Is it the thing with David?” Her tone softened. “Did you guys break up?”

“No. David is fine. David is… unimpeachably fine.” I looked at the soup, which was a drab, brownish green. David was unimpeachably fine. He recycled meticulously. He asked for consent in a way that was both admirable and, lately, faintly eroticizing. He wanted to “co-create a shared life vision.” The problem was, I had no vision to share. Just a static hum, a feeling of living two inches to the left of my own body.

“It’s not David,” I said. “It’s me. I just… need to reset. Unplug.”

“You unplug by coming to visit me and letting my kids cover you in Play-Doh. Not by sitting in a room with a bunch of strangers who aren’t talking. That’s my department meetings.”

I laughed, but it sounded thin. How could I explain the thread? The avocado God? Leah and I had been friends since we were seven, when she’d taught me how to whistle with a blade of grass. She knew every version of me—the leg-shaving girl, the angry undergraduate, the grant-writing professional. But this new, seeking fissure? It felt like a betrayal of our shared, sensible atheism. We believed in science, in systems, in the tangible. Not in whispers from the void.

“It’s just a weekend,” I said, a feeble defense.

She sighed, a sound that carried the weight of two dissertations and a thousand packed lunches. “Okay. Fine. But if they make you wear a robe or give them all your money, I’m sending a rescue squad. Text me when you’re back in cell range. And for God’s sake, take a book.”

The retreat center was called “Sky Meadow,” which promised an openness it did not deliver. It was a cluster of low, shingled buildings hunched in a damp valley, shrouded in late-October mist. The trees were skeletal, their black branches scratching at a grey wool sky. It looked less like a sanctuary and more like the setting for a low-budget horror film.

I parked my dented Civic between a Prius with a “Coexist” bumper sticker and a vintage Volvo wagon plastered with faded feminist icons. The air was cold and smelled of wet leaves and woodsmoke. My stomach clenched. This was a mistake. I should turn around, drive back to the city, order Thai food, and watch something murderous and Scandinavian on Netflix.

“You here for the Vipassana weekend?”

A woman stood on the porch of the main building. She was probably in her sixties, with a wild corona of grey hair and eyes the color of weathered denim. She wore a thick sweater, jeans, and Birkenstocks over wool socks. No robe.

“That’s me,” I said, heaving my duffel bag from the passenger seat.

“I’m Greta. Welcome. Leave your shoes here, your phone in the locked box in the hall, and your expectations at the door.” She said it not as a command, but as a friendly weather report. Chance of inner turmoil, highs in the low 40s.

The inside was warm, lit by lamps and a massive stone fireplace. About twenty people were already there, sitting on cushions or milling quietly. They were mostly women, of varying ages, in the uniform of comfortable seeking: fleece, yoga pants, shapeless linen. A few men sat slightly apart, looking either serenely detached or deeply uncomfortable. The silence was already descending, a physical thing, thick and awkward. We were like actors backstage after the director has yelled, “Don’t talk! Just be!”

Greta rang a small brass bell. The sound was pure, clean, cutting through the muffled room. She spoke in a low, carrying voice. “Welcome. For those new to this practice, Vipassana means ‘to see things as they really are.’ For the next forty-eight hours, we will practice noble silence. No speaking, no reading, no writing, no eye contact. You will follow the schedule. You will meditate. You will eat. You will work. You will rest. The mind will rebel. It will tell you stories, sing you songs, review your greatest failures and your grocery list. Your job is not to stop the mind. Your job is to watch it. To observe the sensations, without attachment, without aversion. Just… notice.”

Notice. The word landed in me like a stone in a pond. I had spent a lifetime analyzing, deconstructing, arguing, becoming. Noticing was passive. It felt like surrender.

We were assigned rooms. Mine was a small cell with a single bed, a desk, and a window that looked out onto the dripping woods. My roommate was already there, a woman about my age with a severe black bob and a tattoo of a labyrinth on her forearm. She gave me a tight, silent nod—the retreat-center equivalent of a hearty handshake—and began meticulously folding her socks into perfect squares. I put my bag on the other bed, sat down, and immediately felt the urge to scream.

The schedule was brutal in its simplicity. 5 AM wake-up bell. Meditation. Breakfast. Meditation. Walking meditation. Lunch. Work period (we were assigned chores; I got bathroom cleaning). Meditation. Dinner. Final meditation. Bed. The meals were vegetarian, eaten in silence in the dining hall, the only sounds the clink of spoons on bowls, the shuffling of feet, the occasional, startling gurgle of someone’s digestive system.

The first meditation session after arrival was in the zendo, a converted barn with polished floors and rows of black cushions. I chose one near the back, cross-legged, spine trying for dignified but settling for strained. Greta sat at the front, a still mountain.

“Begin by focusing on the breath,” she said, her voice a calm river in the quiet. “The natural inhalation. The natural exhalation. When the mind wanders, gently return to the breath.”

I closed my eyes. Inhale. Exhal—Did I turn off the coffeemaker? Inhale. Ex—My lower back hurts. That woman with the labyrinth tattoo probably has a very organized life. I bet she enjoys silence. I should have taken Leah’s advice and brought a book. Any book. The phone book. Inhale. Exhale. What is David doing right now? Probably composing a thoughtful email about our emotional choreography. I should want to marry David. He’s a good man. A feminist man. Why does the thought make me feel like I’m being buried alive?

My mind was a frantic, chattering monkey, swinging from worry to memory to planning to self-loathing. I peeked. Others sat like statues. Greta was motionless. I felt a hot wave of failure. I couldn’t even do silence right.

The bell rang. An hour had passed. It felt like a decade.

Dinner was lentil stew (browner than mine) and dense bread. I ate slowly, trying to just taste, to just feel the spoon in my hand, the warmth in my belly. It was impossible. My thoughts were a torrent. I remembered my mother eating alone at the kitchen table after my father went to bed, the click of her fork against the plate the only sound in the house. A different kind of silence. A silence of surrender.

That night, in the narrow bed, the silence of the room was absolute. My roommate breathed evenly in the dark. The woods outside were still. And inside my skull, the noise was deafening. All the voices: my mother’s grim prophecies, Sam’s fierce certainty, Leah’s pragmatic concern, David’s reasonable plans, the glossy promises of the magazines, the stern dictates of theory. A cacophony of should. A Greek chorus of other people’s scripts.

And underneath it, faint but persistent, was the hum. The thread. The feeling I’d had in the stone church. It wasn’t a voice. It was a lack of voice. A space. A listening.

I cried again, silent tears soaking into the scratchy pillowcase. Not from sadness. From exhaustion. The exhaustion of holding all those voices, all those selves, apart from each other. The feminist, the seeker, the daughter, the lover—they were all at war, and I was the battlefield.

The 5 AM bell was a mercy.

The second day was worse. My body ached. My mind, deprived of its usual distractions, turned in on itself with a vicious creativity. It replayed every mistake, every awkward conversation, every unkind thought. I judged the other retreatants: the man who sighed dramatically every ten minutes, the woman who hummed tunelessly under her breath. I judged Greta for her serenity. I judged myself for judging.

During walking meditation on a muddy path in the woods, the instruction was to move slowly, with deliberate attention to the sensation of lifting, moving, placing each foot. Lift. Move. Place. It was agonizingly boring. My mind raged against the slowness. This is a waste of time. I have reports to write. Emails to answer. A life to manage.

And then, as I placed my foot on a patch of moss, a sudden, vivid memory: I am six years old, in my grandparents’ backyard. I am watching a ladybug traverse a blade of grass. Its shell is a perfect lacquered dome. It moves with infinite, tiny purpose. I am utterly absorbed. The world has shrunk to this journey, this blade of grass, this miniature, spotted pilgrim. My grandmother calls me for lunch, and the spell breaks, but for that moment, I was there. Fully, completely there.

The memory was a shaft of clear light in the murk of my thoughts. It carried no analysis, no lesson. Just the pure sensation of noticing. The feeling of the damp grass under my knees, the smell of cut earth, the focused awe in my own small chest.

Lift. Move. Place.

For three steps, my mind was quiet. I was just a body in the cold air, a foot meeting the earth. The warring voices fell silent. There was just the crunch of leaves, the call of a distant crow, the feeling of my own breath frosting in front of me.

Then, of course, the monkey mind seized it. That was it! A breakthrough! You’re getting it! You should write about this! The spell shattered.

But I had felt it. A crack of pure, unmediated experience. It felt like remembering a language I’d spoken before I was born.

The work period came. I was handed a bucket, a brush, and a bottle of pine-scented cleaner. My assignment: the women’s bathroom. Not the avocado green of my childhood, but a utilitarian space of white tile and chrome. I knelt by the first toilet, the chemical smell sharp in my nose. Resentment rose, bitter and familiar. Is this the divine feminine? Scrubbing toilet bowls in silence? This was my mother’s war, downgraded to a chore.

I scrubbed. The brush made a rhythmic, swishing sound. Back and forth. Back and forth. The motion was mindless, monotonous. My thoughts began to slow, worn down by the repetition. I noticed the pattern in the tile floor. The way the water swirled in the bowl. The ache in my kneeling knees. The coolness of the porcelain under my fingers.

And then, a shift. Not the vast presence of the avocado God. Something smaller, denser. A quiet, gritty attention rising from the task itself. This was also a ritual. This, too, was a practice. Not of becoming, but of being. Being the woman on her knees, cleaning. Not as a martyr, not as a revolutionary, not as a seeker. Just as a body, doing a thing.

A line from a long-forgotten poem surfaced in my mind, unattributed: Before enlightenment, chop wood, carry water. After enlightenment, chop wood, carry water.

I finished the last toilet. The bathroom gleamed, smelling sharply of artificial pine. I emptied the bucket, watching the grey water swirl down the utility sink. There was no epiphany. No voice from the heavens. Just a clean bathroom, and a strange, settled feeling in my chest. A feeling that, for a few minutes, I had not been at war with myself. I had simply been a woman, cleaning a toilet, on a cold afternoon in the Catskills.

That night, the final meditation. I sat on my cushion, my body still protesting. But the mental storm had quieted to a drizzle. I focused on my breath. In. Out. Thoughts came—I’m hungry. My foot is asleep. What will I say to Leah?—but they didn’t hook me. They drifted through like clouds. I watched them come. I watched them go.

In that space between thoughts, in the quiet dark behind my eyelids, I felt it. The thread. It was not a voice giving answers. It was a presence, holding the question. Holding me, the questioner. It held the feminist, the bathroom cleaner, the girlfriend, the daughter, the seeker. It held all of my contradictions not as problems to be solved, but as notes in a chord. A strange, dissonant, uniquely mine chord.

The bell rang, soft and final.

We broke silence the next morning over oatmeal and stewed prunes. The sound of twenty people talking at once was a shocking, joyful cacophony. The woman with the labyrinth tattoo, whose name was Chloe, smiled at me. “The bathrooms have never been so clean,” she said, and it felt like a benediction.

Greta caught my eye as I was loading my duffel into the car. The mist had burned off, revealing a sky of hard, brilliant blue. “So?” she asked. “See anything real?”

I thought of the ladybug. The toilet brush. The quiet chord. “Glimmers,” I said.

She nodded, her denim eyes crinkling. “Glimmers are enough. They’re the cracks the light gets in through. Don’t forget to breathe in the city.”

The drive back was different. The same roads, the same dying autumn landscape, but I was different. I hadn’t found God at Sky Meadow. I hadn’t found peace, or answers. I had found my own noticing. And I had found, to my shock, that the warring voices in my head, when I stopped fighting them and just listened, didn’t want to destroy each other. They just wanted to be heard.

My phone, resurrected from its locked box, buzzed to life as I hit the highway. A string of texts from David. Hope you found what you were looking for! Can’t wait to hear about it. Thinking we should try that new Ethiopian place tonight?

I looked at the words on the screen. The expectation in them. The shared life vision, waiting to be co-created. I felt the old canyon yawn inside me. The self on one side, ready to perform, to narrate a sanitized version of the weekend. The self on the other, holding the quiet, messy glimmer.

I didn’t text back right away. I rolled down the window, letting the cold, traffic-scented air blast in. I turned off the podcast I usually listened to. I drove in silence, listening to the engine, the wind, the sound of my own breath.

Noticing.

CHAPTER 3

The Ethiopian restaurant was all warm, spongy injera and aromatic stews whose names I immediately forgot. David, across the low basket-table, was earnestly dissecting his Doro Wat. “The complexity of the berbere is incredible. You can really taste the fenugreek.”

I tore off a piece of bread, scooped up some lentils. “Mmm.” “So,” he said, wiping his fingers. “The retreat. How was it? Did you… ascend?”

He meant it as a joke, a gentle tease. David’s spirituality was confined to an appreciation for PBS nature documentaries and a vague, civic belief in “the human spirit.” It was one of the things I’d liked about him initially—no mystical baggage to unpack.

“No ascension,” I said, aiming for light. “Mostly just back pain and a startling awareness of my own digestive system.”

He laughed, the right response. “Sounds brutal. But good to unplug, I bet. I went camping once in college. Lasted a day. Missed my coffee maker.”

This was the script. I was supposed to laugh, agree, slip back into the easy rhythm of us. The Maggie who did quirky, slightly stressful things and then came home to her sensible, supportive boyfriend. But the glimmer from Sky Meadow, that quiet chord, was a pebble in my shoe. A constant, subtle reminder of a different rhythm.

“It was… interesting,” I ventured, stepping off-script. “The silence. It’s less about not talking and more about hearing everything else. Yourself, mostly. And not… running from it.”

He nodded, chewing thoughtfully. “Mindfulness. Very healthy. The data on meditation for stress reduction is really compelling.” He reached for his IPA. “Maybe we could try an app? Do it together?”

We. The word landed with a soft, definitive thud. We would mediate with an app. We would get Ethiopian food. We would co-create a vision. The intention was kindness, partnership. So why did it feel like a gentle, loving cage?

“Maybe,” I said, the word ash in my mouth.

Later, in my apartment, the pebble became a boulder. David was washing the dishes—he always washed the dishes—and I was staring at the geraniums on the fire escape, now brown and brittle with frost. The silence between us was of a different quality than the retreat silence. This was a silence full of words unsaid, a dense, polite fog.

“You seem distant,” he said, drying his hands on a towel. He leaned against the counter, his face a mask of compassionate concern. “Was the retreat more intense than you’re letting on?”

He was giving me an opening. A chance to share, to be vulnerable, to co-create. The feminist part of my brain applauded his emotional labor. The part that had sat in the stone church, the part that had scrubbed the toilet in a state of gritty grace, recoiled. This felt like another performance. The Sharing My Feelings performance, with its expected lines and satisfactory resolution.

“I don’t know,” I said, which was true. “It’s just… there’s this feeling I can’t shake. Like I’m living a life that’s one size too small. Or I’m in the wrong play.”

He came over, put his hands on my shoulders. His touch was warm, familiar. “That’s normal, Mags. It’s the Saturn return. Or pre-30s anxiety. We’re figuring it out. Together.”

I looked up at him. He was so good. So decent. He would be an excellent father, a reliable partner. He believed in equality, in therapy, in sustainable living. He was the man my feminist foremothers had fought for me to have the option to choose. And choosing him felt like a betrayal of a self I was only just beginning to meet.

The canyon inside me groaned, widening. On one side, David and the whole sensible, progressive, achievable future. On the other, a whispering, formless void that offered only questions and the terrifying freedom to answer them alone.

“David,” I said, my voice barely a whisper. “I think… I need some space.”

His hands stilled on my shoulders. “Space? What do you mean?”

“Not from you. I mean… from everything. From the ‘we’ of it all. I need to… hear myself think. Without the ‘we’ in the room.”

He removed his hands, took a step back. His expression shifted from concern to confusion, then to a dawning, wounded understanding. “Okay. This is… is this about marriage? Because we never have to get married if you don’t want to. We can just be as we are.”

“It’s not about marriage.” I felt desperate, inarticulate. How could I explain that the problem wasn’t the institution, but the integration? That I needed to let my warring selves battle it out without an audience, without a partner to comfort the victor? “It’s about me. I’m… lost. And I think I need to be lost by myself for a while.”

The word lost hung in the air between us, ugly and final. David, the cartographer of shared life visions, had no map for this.

“So,” he said, his voice tight. “A break.”

It wasn’t a question. I nodded, tears welling. They were for his pain, and for my own cruelty in causing it. “I’m so sorry.”

He didn’t yell. He didn’t plead. He just looked at me, his good, kind face pale under the kitchen’s fluorescent light. “Okay,” he said quietly. He picked up his jacket from the back of a chair. “Take all the space you need, Maggie.”

He left. The click of the door latch was the loudest sound I’d ever heard.

The silence that followed was not noble. It was hollow, sickening. I spent the week in a state of suspended animation. I went to work, wrote grants, attended meetings. I was a ghost in my own life. I called Leah.

“You broke up with David?” she said, after a long pause. “Because of a silent retreat?”

“It wasn’t because of the retreat. The retreat just… clarified things.”

“Clarified what? That you have a great boyfriend who respects you?”

“That I don’t know who I am when I’m not someone’s girlfriend, or someone’s daughter, or someone’s… project.” The word shocked me as I said it. I had been David’s project as much as he had been mine. A joint venture in modern coupledom.

Leah sighed, the professor dealing with a persistently illogical undergrad. “Maggie, no one knows who they are. That’s the human condition. We make it up as we go along, with the people we love. You don’t find yourself in a vacuum.”

“Maybe I need the vacuum.”

“You’ll suffocate,” she said, blunt as always. But then her voice softened. “Look, I love you. You’re my oldest friend. If you need to do this, do it. But don’t romanticize it. Being alone is just that. It’s boring, and it’s hard, and your thoughts are terrible company.”

She was right. The next month was a masterclass in the unbearable weight of my own company. I didn’t have dramatic revelations. I ate toast for dinner. I watched entire seasons of television in a single weekend. I cried at stupid commercials. I tried to meditate, but my apartment was too full of David’s ghost—the ghost of his coffee mug, his side of the bed, his reasonable, caring voice.

The canyon wasn’t a place of insight; it was a ditch, and I was stuck in it.

Desperate, I did the only thing that felt remotely like forward motion. I went back to the stone church.

CHAPTER 4

The stone church in daylight was a different animal. At night, it had been a silhouette, a cut-out against the bruised sky, all promise and shadow. By the gray, flat light of a Tuesday afternoon, it was just a building. A squat, weary-looking thing of flint and mortar, hunched at the end of a gravel drive like an old man waiting for a bus that never came. The sign out front, which I’d missed in the dark, read “St. Ignatius Retreat House” in faded, no-nonsense letters. It did not look like a place where one found God. It looked like a place where one found dampness and weak tea.

I parked my car, the sound of tires on gravel an obscene crunch in the quiet. My heart was a frantic bird in my throat. This was the vacuum. I had asked for it. Now, standing before its unglamorous door, I wanted to turn back, to call David, to apologize for my madness and resume the project of us. Leah’s voice echoed: Your thoughts are terrible company.

I went inside.

The foyer was a shock of warmth and the smell of old wood and lemon polish. It was silent, but a living silence, the kind held by thick walls and absorbed by wool carpets. A woman sat at a plain oak desk, knitting something in a violent shade of purple. She was about sixty, with a cap of steel-gray curls and glasses on a chain. She didn’t look up.

“I’m here for the retreat,” I said. My voice was too loud, a rock thrown into a still pond.

“Check-in’s at four,” she said, her needles clicking a steady, indifferent rhythm. “You’re early.”

“I know. I just… came early.”

She finally glanced up, her eyes magnified and unblinking behind her lenses. They took me in—my city coat, my nervous hands, the whole sorry project of me. “You’re the one from the other night. The one who left.”

“Yes.”

“Father Mark’s in the garden. Through there, out the side door.” She went back to her knitting. The audience was over.

I found the side door and stepped into a walled garden. It was November, and everything was bones and ghosts. Leafless rose bushes were tied to stakes, their stems a thorny script. A stone bench, green with moss, sat under a skeletal birch. And there, kneeling in a patch of turned earth with a trowel in his hand, was the priest. He wore a heavy cardigan over his clericals, and his hands were muddy.

“Hello, Maggie,” he said, without turning around.

“How did you know it was me?”

“You walk like you’re carrying something breakable.” He sat back on his heels and looked at me. His face was calm, weathered. “You came back.”

“I didn’t know where else to go.”

He nodded, as if this was the most reasonable statement in the world. He gestured to the earth beside him. “The last of the bulbs. Tulips. You bury them when the world is dying, and you have to trust they’ll remember to come up.” He handed me a bulb, a small, papery brown teardrop. “Make a hole, about this deep.”

I knelt. The damp of the earth seeped through the knees of my jeans. I took the trowel, its handle smooth from use, and dug. The action was simple, physical. The soil was dark and cold. I placed the bulb in the hole, pointy end up, like he showed me.

“What do I do now?”

“Cover it up. And wait.”

“That’s it?”

“That’s the entire project.” He smiled, a faint creasing around his eyes. “The retreat is silent, as you know. But the silence here… it’s not a punishment. It’s not a vacuum. Think of it as the soil. Things grow in it, but only if you stop digging them up to check.”

I covered the bulb, patting the earth flat. A tiny, meaningless burial. “I don’t know how to be silent.”

“Good,” he said. “Then you might actually learn something.”

The retreatants gathered in the common room at four. There were twelve of us. We were a sad bouquet of human confusion. A silver-haired man with the restless eyes of a retired CEO. A young woman with a tattoo of a bird on her wrist, picking at her cuticles. A middle-aged couple who sat apart, not looking at each other. We were given folders with schedules, rules—no speaking, no eye contact, no phones, no books. Meals were taken in silence. The only structured events were morning prayer in the chapel and an evening “discernment session” with Father Mark, where we could speak if we wished, but most, he said, did not.

My room was a cell. A narrow bed, a desk, a crucifix on the wall, a window overlooking the dead garden. I put my bag on the floor. It contained clothes, a journal, a pen. The artifacts of a person. I sat on the bed. The silence pressed in, not peaceful, but hungry. It ate the sound of the radiator’s clank, the distant cry of a crow. It wanted to eat me, too.

The first challenge was the body. I was not used to inhabiting it without a soundtrack. Without the radio, without the chatter in my own head narrating every moment. My skin felt too tight. My breath was too loud. At dinner in the refectory, the scraping of spoons on bowls was a symphony of agony. The slurp of soup. The clearing of a throat. I focused on the food—a simple vegetable stew, dense brown bread. I tasted each carrot, each pea, with a startling intensity. It was boring, and it was hard, just as Leah had said. My thoughts were terrible company. They circled like vultures: This is stupid. David was right. You are a cliché. You are alone in a room full of alone people. You are a bulb buried in the dark, and you have forgotten how to grow.

The second challenge was the night. In the absolute dark of the countryside, with no streetlights, no glow of a laptop, the darkness was a physical presence. It had weight and texture. I lay in the narrow bed and felt the canyon inside me widen. It was not a ditch. It was a vast, echoing space, and I was falling through it. I saw my mother’s bathroom, the avocado tub. I saw David’s face, kind and confused, as he picked up his jacket. I saw the older girls at the pool, their laughter a soundless movie behind glass. I cried, the sobs absorbed by the pillow. There was no one to hear. It was the loneliest and most honest thing I had ever done.

By the third day, a shift occurred. The boredom didn’t lessen, but my resistance to it did. The silence began to change from an adversary to a medium, like water. I moved through it. I walked the grounds, a labyrinth of gravel paths and bare hedges. I saw the silver-haired man walking too, his pace military-precise. I saw the young woman sitting on a bench, staring at her hands as if reading a map. We were a small, fractured team, assembled not by choice but by desperation. Our unique talent was endurance.

I began to notice my ally. Not a person, but the garden. I returned to it every afternoon. I found the patch where we’d planted the bulbs. I sat on the mossy bench and did nothing. One afternoon, the young woman with the bird tattoo was there. She didn’t look at me. She sat on the far end of the bench, her shoulders hunched against the cold. We sat in parallel silence for twenty minutes. Then, from her pocket, she pulled a handful of birdseed. She scattered it on the frozen ground. Within minutes, sparrows came, then a bold, bright cardinal. We watched them feed, a tiny explosion of life in the monochrome world. It was a communion. She never looked at me, but when she got up to leave, she gave a small, almost imperceptible nod. Our team had solidified. We were the ones who watched.

The proxy for the antagonist came on the fourth day, during the evening discernment session.

We sat in a circle in a room with a fireplace. The flames were the only conversation. Father Mark sat with us, his presence a calm anchor. He asked a simple question into the quiet: “What is the noise you are most afraid to live without?”

The retired CEO spoke first, his voice rusty. “The noise of being needed.” The young woman, her voice a whisper: “The noise of the story I tell about myself. That I’m strong. That I’m fine.” The husband from the couple, not looking at his wife: “The noise of being right.”

Then a man spoke from the shadows near the hearth. I hadn’t noticed him much before. He was in his forties, with a pleasant, forgettable face. He wore a simple wedding band. His voice was soft, reasonable.

“The noise of goodness,” he said.

We all looked at him.

“I’m afraid of the silence,” he continued, “because in the silence, I might hear that I’m not a good person. That my goodness is just… a performance. For my wife, my kids, my friends. I do the right things. I say the right things. But in here?” He tapped his chest. “It’s quiet. And it’s empty. And I’m terrified that the emptiness is the real thing, and the goodness is just the noise I make to cover it up.”

A chill went through me that had nothing to do with the draft from the old windows. He had given voice to the formless dread that had driven me here. The antagonist wasn’t a person or a force. It was the void, the hollow at the center of the performance. The fear that when all the projects were completed—the shaved legs, the successful relationship, the acceptable life—there would be nothing standing there but a woman who didn’t know her own name. His confession laid bare its power and its reach. It lived in all of us. It was the silence beneath the silence.

Father Mark didn’t offer comfort. He simply nodded. “Thank you, Thomas.”

Later, in the hallway, I passed Thomas. He was looking out a window at the black night. He felt my gaze and turned. He gave me a small, weary smile, the smile of a fellow miner trapped in the same shaft. There was no menace in him, only a profound recognition. He was the minion of the emptiness, and he had shown me its face.

The setback came from my own character flaw. I had always been a curator. Of impressions, of experiences, of my own life. I could not let a moment be; I had to capture it, label it, put it on the shelf of my understanding.

On the fifth day, something happened. I was in the garden. The sun had broken through the clouds, a pale, wintery gold. I was watching the light play on the flint wall of the church. I wasn’t thinking. I wasn’t narrating. I was just seeing. The gray stone wasn’t just gray; it was a thousand colors—flecks of silver, veins of blue, patches of lichen the color of dried cream. The world became astonishingly particular. The sound of the wind wasn’t wind; it was the sigh of the birch branches, the rattle of the dry hydrangea heads, the skitter of a last leaf across the gravel. I was inside the sensing, not outside judging it.

A feeling rose in me. It wasn’t happiness, or peace. It was simpler. It was is-ness. A sense of being exactly this creature, in this body, on this patch of cold earth, under this sky. The canyon was still there, but I was not at the bottom. I was a stone on its rim, part of its architecture.

It was a moment of pure, unselfconscious presence. And I ruined it.

My mind, the curator, leapt forward. This is it. This is the insight. This is what you came for. Remember this. Hold onto this. This is the thing that will fix you.

The instant I thought that, the moment shattered. The light became just light. The stone became just a wall. The is-ness vanished, replaced by the familiar, frantic commentary. I had reached for it, and like a soap bubble, it popped at the touch. I had betrayed the silence with my own need to possess its gift. The internal tension was a physical ache. I had been given a taste of the water, and I had tried to bottle it, and now I was thirstier than ever.

I went to Father Mark’s small office after supper. He was reading a battered copy of something in Greek.

“I had it,” I said, my voice raw from days of disuse. “And I lost it.”

He put a scrap of paper in his book and closed it. “Had what?”

“I don’t know. A feeling. That I was real. That everything was real. And then I… I grabbed for it, and it disappeared.”

“Ah,” he said. He leaned back. “You met the experience. Then you met the manager of the experience. The manager always shows up. Its job is to claim the territory, to put up a sign. Maggie Was Here. Maggie Felt Something.”

“How do I fire the manager?”

“You don’t. You just notice when it’s talking. And you go back to the feeling in your feet on the floor. The sound of the wind. The manager hates that. It bores him to death.” He picked up his book again. “The bulb doesn’t think about being a tulip. It just rests in the dark and does its work. Try to be more like a bulb, Maggie. And less like a botanist.”

That night, in my cell, I didn’t try to meditate. I didn’t try to recapture the feeling. I just lay in the dark and listened to the wind. I felt the rough texture of the wool blanket under my fingers. The manager chattered: This is pointless. You’re not getting anywhere. I heard it, the way you hear a radio playing in another room. I didn’t argue. I just returned to the feeling of my breath, moving in, moving out.

It was a minor defeat. I had been shown a country and then had the map torn from my hands. But the memory of the landscape remained. I had, for a second, stepped out of the project of myself. I had been a creature, not a curatorial exhibit. It was a place. And I knew, even if I couldn’t find my way back, that it existed.

On the final morning, we gathered for a last prayer before departing. The silence was about to end. We would return to the world of noise and names and stories. I felt a panic, not of leaving, but of returning. Could I carry any of this with me?

After the prayer, Father Mark stood before us. “You will leave here,” he said, his voice gentle. “And you will forget most of this. The silence will feel like a dream. The world will be very loud, and very convincing. It will tell you who you are, and you will be tempted to believe it.” He looked at each of us. “When that happens, remember the garden. Remember the bulb. It does its work in the dark, unseen. You don’t have to see the growth to trust it is happening. Your job is just to stay in the ground.”

We filed out. I packed my bag. The room was just a room again. I went to the garden one last time. The patch of earth where we’d planted the bulbs was blank, anonymous. Nothing to see.

The young woman with the bird tattoo was at the bench. She saw me and, for the first time, made direct eye contact. She held out her hand. In her palm were two smooth, gray river stones. She offered me one. I took it. It was cold and solid.

“For the pocket,” she said. Her voice was husky. “To remember the weight of quiet.”

“Thank you,” I said. It felt like the first true word I’d spoken in a week.

I put the stone in my coat pocket. I got in my car. As I drove down the gravel drive, the weight of the stone bumped against my thigh with every turn. It was a small, hard fact. A seed of silence.

I drove not toward the city, but away from it. I took a road that wound into the hills, following a impulse I didn’t question. The canyon was inside me, and I was learning to carry it. The manager in my head was already drafting the story of my retreat, the lessons learned, the person I had become. I let him talk. I drove, one hand on the wheel, the other in my pocket, fingers curled around the stone.

The road crested a hill. Below, in a valley, lay a small town I’d never seen before. A river cut through it, a silver seam in the dull landscape. And on the near bank, partly hidden by bare trees, was a building. Long and low, made of timber and glass. A sign, simple and new, stood by the road. I slowed the car.

The sign read: The Stillpoint Collective. Workshops. Residence. Sanctuary.

I pulled over. My heart was not a frantic bird. It was a steady, quiet drum. I looked from the sign to the building, then back at the road ahead, the road that led home to my empty apartment, to David’s ghost, to the life that was a museum of my former projects.

The hook was not dramatic. It was a quiet, insistent pull, like the tug of a fish deep under dark water. I had asked for a vacuum. I had been given soil. And now, here was another door.

I turned the wheel, and my tires crunched onto the dirt drive leading down toward the river.

CHAPTER 5

The Stillpoint Collective was not a building so much as a collection of silences held together by wood and glass. I parked my car on the gravel crescent, the sound of the engine dying away into a quiet so deep it felt like a substance. The river was a low, constant whisper behind the trees. I got out, the cold air sharp in my lungs, and the stone in my pocket was a second, smaller heart beating against my thigh.

A woman emerged from the glass doors. She was maybe sixty, her hair a long, silver braid over one shoulder, her body wrapped in a thick, undyed wool shawl. She moved as if she were listening to the ground with her feet.

“You found us,” she said. Her voice was like the river sound—present, but without insistence.

“I saw the sign.”

“People usually do,” she said, and it wasn’t cryptic, just factual. “I’m Elara. Welcome.”

She didn’t ask my name. She turned and I followed, my boots crunching on the frozen path. The interior was one great room, the ceiling supported by massive beams. A fire crackled in a stone hearth at the far end. The air smelled of woodsmoke and dried lavender. There were no decorations, only a few low benches, stacks of folded blankets, and on the wide-plank floor, a series of worn circles where people had sat, and sat, and sat.

It was the opposite of the canyon retreat. There, the silence had been a curated product, a vacuum sealed in glass. Here, the silence was a living thing. It had grown up between the floorboards. It hung in the rafters like moss.

“We offer residency,” Elara said, standing by the fire. “A week, a month. No schedule. No instruction. Unless you want it. The only rule is to participate in the communal silence from dawn until noon.”

“What does participation look like?”

“It looks like not speaking,” she said, a faint smile at the corner of her mouth. “It looks like listening. To the fire. To the river. To the nothing in your head that is actually something.”

The manager in my head began a cost-benefit analysis. I silenced him by touching the stone in my pocket. I had driven here. The hook was in my lip. I was tired of being a curator of my own emptiness.

“I’ll stay,” I said. “A week.”

Elara nodded, as if she’d heard my decision before I’d made it. She showed me to a small room upstairs, a cell with a cot, a desk, and a window that looked out on the river. It was profoundly simple. It asked nothing of me, not even that I improve.

That first evening, I met the others at the simple supper served in the great room. We ate in silence, as per the custom after noon. There was a man with the weathered face of a fisherman, his hands permanently cupped as if around a missing rope. A young woman who moved with a dancer’s tension, her eyes never settling. An older couple who communicated with glances that had the weight of whole sentences. We were six, including Elara.

We washed our own bowls. The silence was not awkward. It was a shared task. I felt the weight of my own noise, the constant, low-grade hum of narration I carried with me, and for the first time, I recognized it as a form of labor. I was so tired.

The preparation was not for a battle, but for a descent. The resources were not weapons, but permissions.

The next morning, the dawn silence began. I took a blanket and sat in one of the worn circles on the floor, facing the bank of windows that looked onto the river. The others found their places. The fisherman sat straight-backed on a bench. The dancer curled into herself on a cushion. Elara sat by the hearth, perfectly still.

I prepared for the confrontation the only way I knew how: I braced. I set my internal shoulders against the coming boredom, the itch of thoughts, the memory of David’s face, the phantom weight of my phone in my hand. I was a fortress awaiting a siege of nothing.

But nothing came. Or rather, something else came.

The silence here was not an absence. It was a field. And into that field, my thoughts did not charge; they wandered, singly, like disoriented animals. I should check my email. The bathroom tap in my apartment drips. My mother’s birthday is next month. Each thought arose, stood for a moment in the vast, quiet field, and then, finding no purchase, simply lay down and disappeared. I did not fight them. I didn’t have to. The silence was stronger than my will to narrate.

The plan, formulated not by my manager-brain but by some deeper, older part of me, was this: to stop formulating plans. To let the vacuum I had begged for finally exist. To see what rushed in.

It wasn’t peace. It was a kind of neutral alertness. I noticed the way the morning light, pale and winter-thin, lay in a precise quadrilateral on the floorboards. I was not meditating. I was just sitting. And in the sheer, mundane is-ness of it, I felt a confrontation more terrifying than any argument: the confrontation with a world that did not need my story about it. The avocado green tub of my childhood just was. The gray river stone just was. This silence just was. I had spent a lifetime building a becalmed ship of a self inside a hurricane of meaning, and here, there was no hurricane. Just weather.

Scene 2

The journey was a walk. The perils were internal, and they wore the face of the familiar.

After the silent morning, after a lunch where we were allowed to speak (quiet, inconsequential words about the soup, the weather), Elara suggested I walk the river path. “It goes east, into the woods,” she said. “There’s a place where the water gathers in a deep pool before the rapids. It’s a good mirror.”

I put on my coat, the stone a familiar weight, and stepped outside. The path was narrow, trodden into the mud and frost at the river’s edge. The journey felt allegorical immediately—the seeker, the path, the water—and I felt the old impulse to narrate it into significance. I fought it by counting my breaths, by focusing on the physical: the slick, black mud, the skeletal branches of alder reaching over the water, the gunmetal gray of the sky.

The psychological peril was nostalgia. It came not as a sweet sadness, but as a system. As I walked, my mind, deprived of its usual frantic project, began its own retreat. It replayed the canyon: the sterile room, the earnest guides, the young woman with the bird tattoo. It replayed David packing his suitcase, the methodical way he folded his sweaters, a man tidying up the evidence of a life. Each memory was a room I tried to enter, to clean, to rearrange. But the silence of the place followed me. It made them just… things that had happened. Not lessons. Not wounds. Not foundations. Just events, sitting in the field of my awareness like stones.

This was the peril: the utter flattening of my personal drama. Without the soaring music of my own interpretation, the plot of my life felt shockingly simple. A girl shaved her legs. A woman was left. She went to places where people were quiet. The terror was in the simplicity. If my suffering wasn’t a sacred, complex text, what was it? If my seeking wasn’t noble, what was it?

I stopped walking, gripping a tree branch for balance, my breath coming in white gasps. I was not having an epiphany. I was having a failure of meaning. The canyon had been a museum. This was an excavation site, and I was the dirt.

I continued. The path grew steeper, the river louder. The environmental peril became real—the mud gave way to slick, moss-covered rocks. I had to focus on my footing, my hands scrabbling for purchase on frozen stone. The physical struggle was a relief. It was a problem with a clear boundary: fall or don’t fall.

I didn’t fall. I climbed a final rise, and there it was: the pool.

Scene 3

The pool was a bowl of obsidian water, perfectly still where the river widened and deepened before funneling into a chute of white rapids downstream. The trees leaned over it, their bare branches making a cracked cathedral dome. The noise of the coming rapids was a distant roar, a promise of chaos, but here, the water was silent.

I sat on a flat rock at the edge, my legs trembling from the climb. This was the heart of the conflict, and it was perfectly tranquil. The vulnerability did not come as a confession to an ally. My only ally here was the silence, and it was a ruthless friend.

I took the river stone from my pocket. It was warm from my body. I held it in my palm, then leaned forward and placed it gently on the surface of the pool. It didn’t plink. It broke the skin of the water with a soft, almost inaudible puh, and sank without a bubble. I watched it dwindle, a dark shape getting smaller and softer, until it vanished into the blackness.

“For the pocket,” she had said. “To remember the weight of quiet.”

I had carried it. I had felt its weight. And now I had given it back to the quiet. It was not a ritual. It was just something I did.

The vulnerable truth arrived then, not in words, but as a physical sensation. A collapse of a wall I hadn’t known was standing. I had come on this spiritual seeking journey to find an answer, to fix what was broken, to become someone else—someone seamless, someone still. I had wanted to trade the messy project of myself for a finished statue.

Sitting by the black mirror of the pool, I saw the truth: I didn’t want to be still. I wanted the chaos to mean something. I wanted the rapids downstream to have a message for me. I wanted God to be in the whirlwind, or in the still small voice, just so long as He was in it, so long as He was a character in my story.

But what if He wasn’t? What if God was the pool? Not the reflection on its surface, but the deep, cold, wordless holding? What if the spiritual seeker was just another project, like hairlessness, another attempt to shave away the raw, unacceptable fact of being alive?

I put my head in my hands. No tears came. It was a dry, quiet despair. The despair of realizing you have been speaking into a void not because you are unheard, but because there is no one on the other end of the line. There is just the line. The connection is the void.

I sat there until the cold seeped through my jeans and into my bones. The relationship that deepened was my relationship with this emptiness. It was not warm. It was not loving. But it was real. It was the first real thing I had touched since the cold lip of the avocado tub.

I stood, stiff and old. I looked once more at the pool, my face a pale, smudged ghost on its surface. Then I turned and started back down the path.

Scene 4

The entrance was not a fortress gate. It was a doorway inside a dream.

By the time I returned to the Collective, the short winter afternoon was fading. The great room was empty, the fire built up to a roaring blaze. I stood before it, letting the heat thaw my frozen skin, my back to the silent room.

I felt the immense pressure ahead. It was the pressure of the ordinary. Of going back to my room tonight. Of waking to the dawn silence tomorrow. Of continuing to sit in the field of nothing, with no guarantee of harvest. The pressure was to endure the truth I had glimpsed: that sanctuary might not be a place you find, but a stamina you develop. A capacity to sit in the room of your own life without redecorating.

Elara entered, carrying two mugs. She handed one to me. Chamomile, steaming. We stood side-by-side, watching the flames.

“The pool is a fierce mirror,” she said after a while.

“It doesn’t give anything back,” I said, my voice rough from disuse.

“No,” she agreed. “It takes. It takes your stones.”

She looked at me, her eyes reflecting the firelight. “Then you learn to sit empty-handed.”

The hook that pulled me toward the next chapter was not dramatic. It was a quiet, logistical wondering. What does sitting empty-handed look like, day after day? It was not a quest for a grail. It was the question of how to live in the absence of one.

That night, in my small room, I lay awake. The river was a constant whisper. I thought of the stone, resting somewhere in the cold, dark mud of the pool. I thought of the manager in my head, exhausted, finally lying down in that wide field. I thought of David, not with pain, but with a strange, quiet curiosity, like thinking of a character in a book I had closed.

I had come seeking a vacuum and found a soil that refused my seeds. I had come seeking a mountain to climb and found a flat, endless plain. The fundamental shift was this: the seeking itself was the thing to be shed. The spiritual path was not a path to something. It was the act of wearing away the path until you could stand still.

Just before sleep, I had a moment of pure, unadorned perception. The moon had risen, bright and cold. Its light fell through my window and onto the bare floorboards, painting a rectangle of silver on the wood. It was not a metaphor. It was moonlight on floorboards. I watched it until my eyes grew heavy. For those few minutes, I was not a seeker. I was not a story. I was an animal in a room, watching the light change.

And that, I understood as I slipped into the black pool of sleep, was the true entrance. The door to the stronghold of the real. It was unguarded. It was everywhere. And the only way in was to stop trying to find it.

Chapter 6

CHAPTER 6

The plan, like all plans conceived in the clean, dry air of a kitchen over bitter tea, was a paper boat. It was meant for puddles, for the controlled, charming chaos of a rain gutter. We were about to launch it into a hurricane.

It was Sister Margot’s idea. The old convent on the hill, the one with the boarded-up windows and the rusted gate, was not abandoned. It was a repository, she said. A reliquary for things the town had decided, collectively, to forget. The ledgers from the old mill that showed the wages, the parish records with certain baptisms discreetly omitted, the personal effects of residents who had vanished into the softer categories of “gone traveling” or “moved to the city.” And, she believed, the original land deed for the riverfront, including the stretch where the retreat house and the pool sat. The current ownership was a palimpsest, she told us, a document written over another document, the original ink bleeding through in the right light. We needed that first page.

“It’s in the Mother Superior’s old study,” Margot said, her fingers tracing a map she’d drawn on the back of a grocery bag. “Third floor. East wing. The lock is a simple deadbolt, but the door swells in the damp. It will complain.”

David was to be the lookout, stationed at the broken gate with a walkie-talkie we’d found in a drawer, its battery life a matter of faith. I was to go with Margot. I was the younger legs, the eyes that could see in the dim. This was the logic we used. The truth was, I think we both understood I had the most practice with theft. I had been stealing glimpses of a different life since I was nine years old in an avocado tub.

We went at three in the morning, a time chosen for its cinematic emptiness. The world was supposed to be asleep. But the world, I was learning, never fully sleeps. It just closes one eye.

The gate shrieked like a wounded bird, a sound that seemed to hang in the wet air long after we’d slipped through. The path up to the convent was overgrown, the gravel long since swallowed by moss and creeping ivy. The building loomed, a great ship run aground, its dark windows like blind eyes. Margot moved with a surprising, feral grace, her habit hitched up around her knees. I followed, my heart a frantic moth in my throat.

The service door was where she said it would be. The lock yielded to a heavy, old-fashioned key she produced from a pocket. The door did, indeed, complain—a long, low groan that seemed to travel up through the stone and into the bones of the place. The smell that rushed out was not simple decay. It was the smell of trapped time: dust, yes, and mold, but also beeswax, old wool, and the faint, ghostly trace of lily of the valley perfume.

We used penlights, the beams cutting frail tunnels in the absolute dark. The foyer was a cathedral of shadows. A staircase swept upwards, its banister thick with dust. Our footsteps were blasphemies in the silence. Up we went, the stairs creaking under our weight. First floor. Second. The air grew colder, thinner. The third-floor hallway was a tunnel, doors lining it like sealed tombs.

“East wing,” Margot whispered, her breath a cloud. “This way.”

We turned. And that is when the plan, our paper boat, met its first wave.

A light flicked on at the far end of the hall. Not the weak yellow of a bulb, but the cold, blue-white beam of a modern flashlight, powerful enough to pin us where we stood.

“I thought you said it was empty,” I hissed, my voice barely a sound.

“It is,” she breathed, her face pale in the backglow. “It should be.”

A figure stepped into the periphery of the beam. Not a ghost. A man in a dark jacket. Then another. They moved with a quiet, professional coordination that had nothing to do with caretaking. One of them spoke, his voice flat, echoing in the hollow space.

“Sister Margot. You’re out late.”

I knew that voice. It was the manager from my head, but given flesh. It was the man from the county zoning office who had visited the retreat house last week, asking polite, pointed questions about unpermitted structural repairs. His name was Albrecht. He had smiled with his teeth.

Margot drew herself up. “This is consecrated ground, Mr. Albrecht. You have no authority here.”

“Consecrated to what, exactly?” the other man said. He was younger, with the close-cropped hair of a soldier. “Seems pretty deconsecrated to me. Seems like a fire hazard.”

Albrecht’s beam traveled to me. “And you brought a guest. The seeker from the river house. Looking for something?”

The spiritual path is not a path to something. It was the act of wearing away the path until you could stand still. The theory was beautiful. The practice, in that moment, was to feel every path inside me evaporate, leaving only the animal freeze of prey.

“Run,” Margot said.

It was not a dramatic cry. It was a simple, clear directive, the kind she might use to tell a novice to fetch more candles. Then she stepped forward, directly into the beam of the flashlight, her arms spread slightly as if to block the light itself.

I ran.

I turned and fled back down the dark hallway, away from the east wing, away from the Mother Superior’s study and its bleeding ink. I heard a sharp exclamation, a scuffle, but I didn’t look back. My penlight was a frantic, bouncing star. I hit the staircase and took the steps two, three at a time, my hand skidding on the dusty banister. A shout echoed from above. Footsteps, heavy and fast, pursued.

The plan was in tatters. The team was scattered. The metaphor had shifted: I was no longer launching a boat. I was a rat in a wall, and the house was being demolished around me.

I burst onto the second-floor landing and veered left, into another corridor. My breath sawed in my lungs. The flashlight beam from above swept across the stairwell behind me. I tried a door. Locked. Another. Locked. The third opened, and I fell through, closing it softly behind me, plunging myself into a blackness so complete it felt solid.

I stood with my back against the door, listening. The footsteps pounded down the stairs, paused on my landing. I heard Albrecht’s voice, muffled but clear. “Check the west wing. She can’t get far.”

Their footsteps receded. I waited, counting my heartbeats. When I could no longer hear them, I fumbled for my penlight and clicked it on.

I was in a dormitory. A long, narrow room lined with simple iron bedframes, stripped of mattresses. A large, leaded glass window at the far end allowed a sickly wash of moonlight to pool on the floorboards. It looked like the room from my vision, but corrupted, empty. This was not a place for animals to watch the light change. This was a place where light came to die.

I moved forward, my beam picking out details: a dusty spiderweb strung between bedposts, a single, forgotten hairpin on a windowsill, a water stain on the ceiling that spread like a map of an unknown continent. I was at the window, looking out at the moonlit hills, when I heard the soft click behind me.

The door opened. Not Albrecht or his companion. A woman stood there, backlit by the faint hall light. She was tall, dressed in a simple, dark dress, her hair a silver cap. She held no flashlight. She simply looked at me.

“You are in the wrong room,” she said. Her voice was dry, precise, like pages turning.

“I’m sorry,” I stammered, the inanity of it ringing in my ears. “I was… lost.”

“No one comes here unless they are lost,” she said. She stepped inside, closing the door. The room felt smaller. “You are with the sister. You are looking for the deed.”

I said nothing. My hand tightened around the penlight.

“It isn’t in the study,” she said. She walked slowly along the line of beds, her fingers trailing over the iron footboards. “They moved it years ago. When they realized some memories have a half-life. When they realized a piece of paper can be a stone that sinks a different kind of pool.”

“Who are you?” I asked.

“I was the archivist,” she said. “Now I am the archive.” She stopped and turned to me. Her face was all angles and shadows, her eyes deep-set. “Sister Margot believes in documents. She believes truth is a thing that can be held. I believed that once. I spent my life ordering it, cataloging it. Then I learned that the most important truths are the ones they leave out of the ledgers. The silences between the entries. The names that aren’t there.”

She pointed to the water stain on the ceiling. “That leak started the year the mill foreman’s daughter disappeared. They said she ran off with a salesman. The stain grew when the Johnson boy drowned in the river, though they ruled it an accident. It spread to its current size the winter the county surveyor, a man who asked too many questions about watershed rights, had his car go off the road in a blizzard.” She looked back at me. “This building is a body. Its wounds are a more accurate record than anything in the filing cabinets.”

I understood then. This was the trial. Not a physical fight, not an escape through a window. This was the central enemy: the past itself, not as a story, but as a living, breathing, corrosive silence. And I was alone with its keeper.

“Why are you telling me this?” I asked.

“Because you are a seeker,” she said, not unkindly. “And you have reached the point in the seeking where you must choose. You can continue to look for the stone—the deed, the document, the proof. Or you can understand that the pool is not something you throw stones into. The pool is the stone. It is the weight of all the unspoken things. It is the collective decision to forget. You cannot retrieve a stone from it. You can only recognize that you are already at the bottom, looking up.”

It felt like a death. The death of the quest. The death of the idea that I could do something, that I could fix this by finding the right piece of paper, by winning. Sister Margot was probably captured. David was waiting at a gate for a signal that would never come. The deed was a phantom. I had come here to act, to finally move, and I had been led to a dead room to be told that action was the last vestige of my delusion.

I sank onto the edge of an iron bedframe. The cold metal seeped through my jeans. The symbolic death was not a capture or a physical blow. It was this: the total annihilation of hope in the form I had given it. The project was over. I had run out of stones to give. And I did not know how to sit empty-handed.

“What do I do?” The question was a whisper, a child’s question.

The archivist came and sat beside me, not too close. She smelled of lavender and old paper. “You stop looking for the door,” she said. “You are already inside the stronghold of the real. It is this. This room. This failure. This silence. It is unguarded because no one wants it. It is the truth that offers no advantage, that wins no argument, that secures no deed. It simply is.”

We sat in the quiet for a long time. The moon traveled across the window. The rectangle of silver light slid across the floor, over the dust, over the forgotten hairpin. I watched it. I was not a seeker. I was not a thief in the night. I was an animal in a room, watching the light change, with the weight of the entire town’s unspoken history sitting beside me.

And then, from that stillness, the insight came. It did not arrive with a fanfare. It was not an unexpected rescue. It was a rearrangement of what was already there.

The pool takes your stones. And when you run out, you learn to sit empty-handed.

But what if you didn’t just sit?

What if you understood that your empty hands were not a sign of poverty, but of a different kind of capacity? A vessel, not for holding, but for receiving. The pool was a mirror that didn’t give back. But a mirror, if you stop throwing things at it, just reflects what is in front of it.

I stood up. The archivist watched me, her face unreadable.

“They moved the deed,” I said. “But you know where.”

“I know where they put the box,” she corrected.

“Will you show me?”

“It will not give you what you think it will.”

“I’m not sure what I think anymore,” I said. And it was true. The seeking was shed. What was left was not certainty, but a clear, cold direction. Action, but of a different species. Not the paper boat, but the current itself.

She nodded, once, and rose. She led me out of the dormitory, not back to the main hall, but to a small, plain door I had taken for a closet. It opened onto a narrow, twisting servants’ staircase, stone steps worn smooth in the centers. We descended, deep into the belly of the convent, further than I thought possible. The air grew damp and earthy.

We emerged in a cellar. It was not a dungeon, but a root cellar, lined with empty, rotten shelving. In the far corner, behind a collapsed rack, was a small, iron-bound chest, no larger than a shoe box.

“There,” she said.

I knelt before it. The lock was simple, rusted shut. I took my penlight, the metal cylinder solid in my hand, and struck the latch once, twice. It gave with a crunch. I lifted the lid.

Inside were not sheaves of paper. There was a single, folded document, crisp and official-looking. The deed. And beneath it, resting on the aged wood of the chest, was a smooth, river-worn stone. Grey, with a single white quartz vein running through it like a lightning bolt.

I picked up the deed. I unfolded it. I read the legal description, the names, the dates. It was all there. The proof. The stone that could sink their project. I felt nothing. No triumph. No relief. It was just paper.

Then I picked up the stone. It fit perfectly in my palm, cool and heavy. The true weight.

The archivist was gone. I hadn’t heard her leave. I was alone in the cellar with a piece of paper and a stone.

I knew what to do.

I left the deed in the chest. I closed the lid. I put the stone in my pocket.

When I emerged from the servants’ entrance into the overgrown garden, the sky was beginning to lighten to a dull pewter grey. I could see the gate. I could see David’s silhouette, pacing. And I could see Albrecht and the other man, standing with their backs to me, talking to him, their postures aggressive, demanding.

I didn’t run. I didn’t hide. I walked. My empty hands swung at my sides. One of them held the penlight. The other was just a hand.

Albrecht heard my footsteps on the gravel and turned. His face showed surprise, then a slick, professional calm. “There she is. We’ve been looking for you. Sister Margot is having a nice chat with the sheriff. I think you should join her.”

I stopped a few feet from them. David’s eyes were wide with warning and fear.

“I wasn’t lost,” I said. My voice was quiet, but it carried in the damp dawn air. “I was in the archive.”

Albrecht’s smile tightened. “Find anything interesting?”

I reached into my pocket. His eyes tracked the movement, his body tensing. I pulled out the stone. I held it up in the grey light. The quartz vein caught what little illumination there was and glowed faintly.

“Just this,” I said.

He stared at it, his confusion genuine. It was the wrong artifact. It broke his script. “A rock?”

“A stone,” I corrected. “From the bottom of the pool.”

I saw it then, the flicker in his eyes. Not fear, but a deeper, more primal recognition. He knew about the pool. He knew what it was for.

“You’re trespassing,” the younger man said, recovering. “We’ll be taking you in.”

“You can try,” I said. I didn’t move. I just stood there, holding the stone, looking at them. I was not threatening them with it. I was simply showing them what I had. What I now carried. The weight of the unspoken thing. The truth that offers no advantage, but simply is.

It was Albrecht who broke. He looked from the stone to my face, and he saw something there—not defiance, but a profound, unsettling stillness. The stillness of someone who has stopped throwing stones. The stillness of someone who has finally seen the mirror for what it is.

“Forget it,” he said to his partner, his voice low. “She’s crazy. Let the sheriff deal with her later.”

He turned and walked away, back through the gate. The younger man hesitated, threw one last, confused glance at the stone in my hand, and followed.

David rushed over. “What happened? Where’s Margot? What is that? Did you get it?”

I looked at his anxious, beloved face. “I got what I came for,” I said. I closed my fingers around the stone. It was not a trophy. It was a reminder. A paperweight for the soul.

The hook that pulled me toward the next chapter was not about what I would do with the deed I’d left behind, or how we would free Margot. It was about the stone in my pocket. It was about the weight of it, a familiar, personal gravity. What do you build, I wondered, when your foundation is no longer a seeking, but a stone? What kind of house stands on that?

Chapter 7

CHAPTER 7

The stone in my pocket was the size of a hen’s egg and weighed as much as the world. It wasn’t the deed. The deed was paper, a flimsy ghost of ownership, a claim staked in ink that could blur or burn. This was different. This was a fact. I walked back to David’s truck with it pressing against my thigh, a dull, specific ache. The quartz vein, that faint, captured light, felt like a nerve exposed to air.

David drove, his knuckles pale on the wheel. The silence between us was not empty; it was full of the words we were not saying. He kept glancing at my closed hand in my lap.

“They just left?” he finally asked, the question he’d been holding since the gate.

“They saw what they needed to see.”

“Which was what?”

I looked out the window at the pines blurring past, a wall of indifferent green. “That I wasn’t playing their game. That I had changed the rules.”

“By showing them a rock.”

“By showing them I’d been to the bottom of the pool,” I said. The words hung there. The pool. That concrete mouth, that liquid silence. I had gone down for a piece of paper and come up with a piece of the earth. It felt like a trade. A sacrament.

We didn’t go back to the motel. We drove to the diner on the county line, a place called The Skillet with chrome trim and vinyl booths the color of dried blood. It was the kind of place where the coffee tasted of tin and the waitresses called you “hon” without seeing you. We needed neutral ground. We needed fluorescent lights and the smell of grease. We needed a world that was brutally, blessedly ordinary.

Margot was already there, wedged into a corner booth. Seeing her was like seeing a reflection in a cracked mirror. Her face was drawn, the elegant architecture of her cheekbones too stark, but her eyes were clear. She had a cup of tea in front of her, steam rising in a thin, persistent plume. She’d gotten my text from the truck: Skillet. Now.

David slid in beside her, his body angled toward her as if to shield her from the room. I sat across from them. The vinyl sighed under my weight.

“You’re okay?” David asked her, his voice low.

“I’m intact,” Margot said. Her gaze was on me. “Albrecht’s men came to the library. They asked very polite questions about archival procedures. They had badges. It was all very official. I gave them nothing but citations.” A ghost of a smile touched her lips. “They don’t like women who know more than they do.”

“They followed you,” I said.

“Of course they did. I led them on a lovely tour of municipal planning records from 1972. They were bored into a stupor by the time your message came.” She leaned forward. “But you. You went into the well.”

“I went into the pool.”

Her breath caught, just slightly. She understood the distinction. The well was a mechanism. The pool was an altar. “And?”

I didn’t answer with words. I opened my hand, which had been curled in a fist on the Formica tabletop. I set the stone between the sugar dispenser and a bottle of ketchup.

It looked absurd there. A piece of creek bed on a checked tablecloth. A primal thing in a place built for patty melts and cherry pie.

Margot didn’t touch it. She bent her head, studying it. Her hair fell forward, a dark curtain. She looked at it the way a scholar looks at a dead language. “This is not the deed.”

“No.”

“This is from the drain.”

“Yes.”

David stared at it, then at me. “I don’t understand. You risked… all of that. For a rock.”

“It’s a keystone,” Margot whispered. She looked up at me, and in her eyes I saw the same recognition Albrecht had shown, but purified, sharpened by intellect. “Not for a building. For a belief. They lined the pool with them. A circle of them, around the drain. A ring of silence.” She reached out, one finger hovering just above the quartz seam. “To hold the water in. To hold the intention down. To make the seeking heavy.”

That was it. The weight in my pocket. It was the weight of all that focused, desperate wanting. The thousands of whispers into dark water. Show me. Tell me. Make me. The stone had absorbed them, a geological confessional.

“So it’s, what, contaminated?” David asked, his voice tight with a practical man’s dread.

“It’s saturated,” I said. “It’s full. It’s the opposite of empty.” I picked it up again. It was warm from my skin. “The deed was a trick. A lure. This is the prize. The thing you’re left with when the water is gone.”

The waitress came then, a woman with tired eyes and a name tag that read “Darlene.” We ordered food we wouldn’t eat. Coffee we would drink too quickly. We performed the ritual of normalcy. When she left, the silence returned, but it was different now. We were looking at the stone, and the stone was looking back.

“What do you do with it?” David asked. The question was enormous.

“You carry it,” I said. And I knew, as I said it, that this was the first beat of my new life. Not a search, but a carrying. I had spent so long throwing stones into the dark, listening for a splash that never came. Now I held the stone that had been waiting at the bottom. The answer to every thrown question is not a sound. It is the impact. The thing you hit.

We ate in brittle, quiet mouthfuls. The celebration, if you could call it that, was a muted thing. It was the celebration of survivors who have made it to a temporary shore, who can feel the tide still pulling at their ankles. David’s relief was a physical presence, a loosening in his shoulders every time he looked at Margot, whole and unharmed. But his eyes would drift to the stone, which I had placed on the seat beside me, and the confusion would tighten his jaw again. He was a man of actions and consequences, of maps and deeds. This spiritual geology was a language he didn’t speak.

Margot spoke it fluently. Between sips of cooling tea, she wove theories. “The Order didn’t just build a reflecting pool. They built a battery. A capacitor for longing. The human desire for transcendence is a form of energy. Wild, diffuse. They channeled it. They used the architecture, the solitude, the ritual of the walk, the finality of the throw—they used it all to focus that energy into the water, down through the drain, into these stones.” She tapped a nail against the tabletop. “They were farming revelation. But not for the seekers. For themselves. The deed was the fence around the pasture. This,” she nodded to the stone, “is a piece of the soil. The fertile, exhausted soil.”

Her words made a terrible sense. It explained the hollow feeling I’d had after every visit to the pool. The depletion. I had been a crop. We all had.

“So the power… the ‘sight’… it’s in these rocks?” David asked, trying to nail down the supernatural into something he could grasp.

“No,” I said. The understanding came not as a thought, but as a certainty, rising from the weight in my hand. “The power was the seeking itself. The stone is what’s left when the seeking is done. It’s the residue. The spent fuel.” I thought of the first time I saw God, in that avocado bathroom. I had been seeking then, too—not God, but smoothness, womanhood, a way out of my own skin. All seeking is the same prayer. Make me other than I am. The stone was the answer: You are what you are. You are this.

“Then it’s worthless,” David said, but without conviction.

“It’s priceless,” Margot countered. “It’s proof. It’s the core sample of their entire operation. With this, and the ledger pages, we don’t just have a legal case. We have a theological one. We can expose the mechanism.”

That was the celebration. The brittle, tempered hope of it. We had a weapon. Not a blade, but a key. A key to a door we hadn’t fully seen yet. We clinked coffee mugs, a hollow, tinny sound. The victory was real, but it tasted of ash and weak coffee. We had stolen the church’s cornerstone, and now we had to figure out what kind of house we were building with it.

The cost revealed itself an hour later, in the parking lot under a burned-out sodium light.

The stone was in my jacket pocket. I felt it with every movement, a new center of gravity. As we walked toward David’s truck, I felt a strange, pulling sensation, not in my body, but in my perception. It was like walking through a spiderweb—a faint, clinging resistance against my mind.

I stopped.

“What is it?” David asked, his hand going to the truck’s door handle.

I looked around the lot. Empty. A few other cars, a dumpster smelling of stale fried food. The night was clear, cold. But the feeling persisted. A pressure. A silent, humming attention.

“It’s listening,” I said.

“What is?”

“Everything.” The word was inadequate. It wasn’t that the dumpster or the asphalt was listening. It was that the attention in the stone was acting like a beacon. It had been a receptacle for decades. Now, removed from its silent choir, it was a solo voice, humming a note only certain ears could hear. I was holding a tuning fork for the invisible world.

Margot understood first. “The connection isn’t broken. You’ve taken the keystone, but the arch is still standing. You’ve just… unbalanced it. The pool is now incomplete. And it’s calling to its missing piece.”

“Or,” David said, his voice grim, “the shepherds are calling for their lost sheep.”

That’s when the first car pulled in. Not Albrecht’s sedan. An older, boxy Volvo, the color of dried moss. It parked at the far end of the lot. No one got out.

Then a second car. A pickup truck with a light bar. It parked perpendicular to the diner’s entrance, not blocking it, but suggesting the idea.

My skin went cold. This wasn’t the polite, badge-flashing intimidation of Albrecht. This was something slower, older. This was the congregation.

“Get in the truck,” David said, his voice low and urgent.

We moved. Margot slid into the passenger seat. I went for the back door. As I pulled it open, I looked back at the diner. Through the plate glass window, I saw Darlene the waitress, standing at the register. She wasn’t looking at her receipts. She was looking out at us. And she was on the phone.

They weren’t following us. They were already here. They were always here. The Order wasn’t just a few men in suits. It was the waitress, the farmer, the mechanic. It was the land itself. We hadn’t stolen from an organization. We had taken a relic from an ecosystem.

David started the engine. The headlights cut across the two waiting vehicles. In the Volvo’s windshield, I saw the faint red glow of a cigarette.

“Don’t speed,” I said from the backseat. “Drive normally.”

He pulled out of the lot, turning onto the county road. The headlights of the pickup truck flicked on, bright and high. It pulled out after us, maintaining a distance of three car lengths.

“Okay,” David breathed. “Okay. We’re okay. We just go back to the motel, get our things, and go.”

But the stone in my pocket hummed. It was a compass needle, and it wasn’t pointing north. It was pointing back. Back to the valley. Back to the pool. It had a gravity, and it was pulling on the things that knew its frequency.

The Volvo fell in behind the pickup. A little convoy of quiet pursuit.

“They’re not trying to stop us,” Margot observed, her voice clinical, detached. “They’re herding.”

The road unspooled, a black ribbon through the sleeping fields. The motel was ten miles away. We had a head start. We could pack in five minutes. The plan was simple. It was a good plan.

It was the wrong plan.

I felt it in the stone. A sudden, sharp tug, like a fishhook set deep in my sternum. I gasped.

“What?” David’s eyes found mine in the rearview mirror.

“Don’t go to the motel,” I said. The certainty was absolute, cold. “They’ll be there. Or it will be. The imbalance… it’s drawing them. It’s drawing things.”

“What things?” David’s knuckles were white again.

“The things that drink from that kind of silence.” I saw the pool not as a concrete bowl, but as a wound. A quiet, open wound in the world. And I had just peeled the scab off. The stone was the scab. And now the wound was weeping into the dark.

The pickup truck behind us sped up, closing the gap by one car length. A signal.

“Where, then?” Margot asked. She was calm. She had accepted we were in a story now, a story with its own logic.

I closed my eyes. I let the pull of the stone guide me. It wasn’t a voice. It was a slope. And I was at the top, beginning to slide. “The old state route. The one that follows the river north. Into the national forest.”

“That’s a dead end. It’s gated, miles in,” David said.

“It’s high ground,” I said. And it was. Spiritually. The river was a vein of chaos, of noise. The forest was a tangle of life. It would be harder to hear the stone’s hum there. It would be harder for them to hear it. We needed to get lost in a louder, wilder kind of quiet.

David swore, softly. Then he took the next left, away from the motel, away from town, onto a narrower road that plunged into a tunnel of overhanging trees. The headlights of our followers swung in behind us.

David shifted, the hatchet glinting. “That’s close enough.”

The Shepherd ignored him. His whole world had funneled down to the space between him and me. “You think it’s a gift. It’s not. It’s a solvent. It dissolves will. It dissolves want. It dissolves the you out of you. That is its purpose. That is the only peace. You are clinging to a rope that is meant to unravel you.”

I remembered the hook in my heart, the relentless pull away from. I remembered the blissful nullity of the fog, the sweetness of surrender. He was right. That was one path. The stone offered unmaking. The Shepherd was its priest.

“You’re wrong,” I said, and the root within me thickened, a quiet pulse. “It’s not a solvent. It’s a graft.”

He blinked. The word meant nothing to him. His theology was one of erasure. Mine, I realized in that moment, was one of integration.

“You give it back,” he whispered, and the whisper was more threatening than a scream. “You give it back, and you walk out of here. You go back to your roads and your radios and your mirrors. You can keep your noisy self. This place… this silence… it is for the ones who are done with being selves.”

This was the battle. Not of force, but of definition. What was the silence for? Was it a destination, or a foundation?

“I am done,” I said, taking a step toward him. I felt David’s tension spike behind me. “But I’m not done into nothing. I’m done into something else. You use the quiet to escape. I am learning to live inside it.”

He snarled, a feral, ugly sound. “You infect it!”

He lunged then. Not for me, but to the side, toward the pool. His movement was jerky, desperate. He scooped his hands into the black water and hurled a great sheet of it toward our fire.

It fell short, hissing on the hot stones at the fire’s edge, sending up a plume of steam. But the intention was clear. He would quench our light. He would drown our little hearth in the cold, indifferent dark he worshipped.

David moved. He didn’t charge. He simply stepped into the Shepherd’s path as he bent for another scoop. “No,” David said, his voice low and solid as the hatchet handle in his grip.

The Shepherd straightened. He looked from David to me, his vacuum eyes flickering with a raw, human hatred. It was the noisiest thing I had ever seen on him. “You’ve made a pet of the practical man,” he sneered. “Does he know what you carry? Does he know it will unmake him, too? That his precious practicality will rot from the inside out until he’s just another still thing in the woods?”

“She’s not unmade me yet,” David said, without turning around. “And you’re not touching that water again.”

The Shepherd’s face contorted. He was a prophet whose only convert was himself, facing heretics who had rewritten the scripture. His power was in persuasion, in the seduction of surrender. Against direct, physical refusal, he was just a man. A furious, empty man.

It was not a calculated attack. It was a collapse. A last, violent attempt to prove his thesis: that all things must be dissolved into the quiet. If he could not have the stone, he would dissolve me, by force, into the nothing he understood.

As he closed the last few feet, hands clawed, spittle flying, I did not see a monster. I saw a boy. He had mistaken the void for a womb.

At the moment before impact, I did not raise my hands to fight. I did not step aside.

The single, pure note.

It did not come from my throat. It came from the root, from the graft, from the place where the stone’s silence and my soul had woven together. It was not loud. It was not a weapon. It was an announcement. A statement of being.

Here.

The Shepherd staggered as if struck in the chest. He fell to his knees, not two feet from me, his hands going to his ears. But the sound wasn’t in the air. It was in the fabric of things. It was in the vibration of the pine needles, in the heartbeat of the pool, in the slow, patient growth of the moss.

He was right. His silence was an empty cup. Mine was an overflowing well. He could not contain it. It was not a solvent; it was a nourishing flood, and it was drowning him.

He was afraid of the sheer, teeming something of a world that was not empty, but profoundly, intricately alive.

“What did you do?” he breathed, not in fear, but in awe.

“I answered,” I said.

I knelt before him. He flinched, hiding his face.

He shook his head, a frantic, childlike motion against the forest floor. “No shape. No shape.”

There was no victory in defeating him. Only sadness. He was a ghost who preferred his haunting. The cleansing was not a battle. It was a release.

I stood. I looked at David. “We need to break camp.”

He understood. We could not stay here with this broken icon of the void. It would be a cruelty to him, and a weight on our new peace.

It was the smooth, worry-worn pebble I had carried from the world before. The last token of the girl in the avocado bathroom. I placed it on a flat stone beside the dying fire. A leaving. A shedding.

I turned to go. But as I took my first step, the Shepherd’s hand shot out. His fingers, cold and damp, closed around my ankle.

I froze. David tensed.

The Shepherd’s face was still pressed to the earth. But his grip was not violent. It was a plea. With a shuddering effort, he turned his head just enough for one eye to be visible. It was no longer a vacuum. It was just an eye, bloodshot, full of a pain so old it had worn smooth.

“The word,” he rasped. “Say it… again.”

He was not asking for an end. He was asking for a beginning. However terrible, however terrifying. He was asking for the note.

I looked at David. He gave a slow, almost imperceptible nod.

I closed my eyes. I felt the stone, the root, the graft. I felt the mildew and the Jean Naté, the headlights and the fog, the sacrifice and the cold pool water. I felt it all, not as a weight, but as soil. My soil.

I opened my eyes, and I spoke the word again, directly to him.

Here.

It was softer this time. A seed, not a sun.

His grip on my ankle tightened for a second, a spasm. Then his fingers went slack. He did not thank me. He did not smile. He simply rolled onto his back, staring up at the canopy where the morning sun was now stitching gold through the green. He was not dissolved. He was, for the first time, simply located. The horror of it was on his face. And beneath the horror, the faintest, most fragile tremor of something else. The possibility of awe.

We walked away. We left him there, by the black mirror of the pool, under the vast and speaking sky. We did not look back.

The rebirth was not mine. It was his. And in granting it, I felt my own final ghost settle. The girl who needed to shave away her self was gone. The woman who was a specific, rooted thing in a specific, speaking world walked on.

For hours, we moved in companionable silence. The forest welcomed us. It was no longer a test. It was a home. The light through the trees was a kind of grace, dappled and gentle.

As dusk began to gather again, we found a new place. A small clearing where a creek chuckled over stones. We did not speak of building a fire. We simply sat, our backs against a fallen log, and shared a piece of hard cheese and some nuts.

David finally broke the quiet. “The thing in your chest,” he said, not looking at me. “It’s growing, isn’t it?”

I put my hand over my heart. I could feel it, not just the stone’s warmth, but the subtle, sure expansion of the root system. It was weaving through the chambers of my heart, threading along my bones, a delicate, silent lattice. “Yes.”

“What happens when it’s done?” he asked. There was no fear in the question. Only a practical curiosity.

“I don’t know,” I said. And for the first time, the not-knowing was not a cliff edge, but a horizon. “A tree doesn’t know what it will look like when it’s full-grown. It just knows how to grow.”

He nodded, chewing thoughtfully. “Good.”

We sat until the stars pricked through the velvet of the sky. The creek’s song was a lullaby. I felt whole, and yet porous. A finished project that was also a perpetual beginning.

Just before sleep took me, leaning against David’s solid shoulder, I felt a new sensation. Not from the stone. Not from the root.

From the world.

A kind of… answering pressure. Not a sound, but a silent, resonant attention. As if my spoken word had been a stone dropped in a pond far vaster than I knew, and the ripples had finally reached a distant, waiting shore. It was not threatening. It was patient. And it was aware.

The door was open. I was home.

And something in the deep, green heart of the world had just heard me come in.

Show me.

“Okay,” I said.

He nodded, and then he was gone, his truck lights cutting twin swaths through the night before being swallowed by the trees. I locked the door. The sound of the bolt sliding home was a satisfying, final note. I was not locking the world out. I was defining the terms of its entry.

CHAPTER 27

Scene 1

Scene 2

The girl arrived just after lunch. She was maybe nineteen, all sharp angles and wide, hungry eyes, sent to me by a professor at the state university who knew of my “ethnobotanical work.” A polite term for what I did: I paid attention.

I made us tea—nettle and lemon balm from the garden. I listened until she ran out of breath.

“Come outside,” I said.

We walked the path. I showed her the garlic cloves, their papery skins like forgotten prayers, pointed downward in the furrows. “They have to go into the dark and the cold,” I said. “They have to forget they’re a clove for a while. She frowned, skeptical. “But how do you know? How do you access it?”

I stopped at the hellebore bed. The flowers were like shy, downward-facing cups, creamy white edged with plum. “It sounds so… ordinary,” she said, disappointment heavy in her voice.

“It is,” I said, standing, my knees popping. “That’s the mountain high enough. Not transcending the ordinary. Learning to live in it. To love the smell of mildew and Jean Naté, and the smell of cold earth. They’re not so different.”

I handed her a trowel and a basket of daffodil bulbs. “Here. The seeking is in the hands. Plant these. Pointy end up. Don’t think about God. Think about six inches deep and four apart.”

For an hour, she worked in silence beside me. The frantic energy in her hands slowly stilled into a rhythm: dig, place, cover. The simple sacrament of burial and hope. When she stood to leave, dirt smudged on her jeans, she looked less like a fledgling priestess and more like a tired, capable young woman.

“Thank you,” she said, and it wasn’t for the tea. “The daffodils will be up in March,” I said. “You can come see them.” She nodded, a new kind of hunger in her eyes—not for escape, but for return.

Scene 3

The letter came with the afternoon mail. The handwriting was precise, familiar. Dr. Silas. My breath caught, not with fear, but with a dull, old ache, like a bone that knows rain is coming.

I took it to the bench under the apple tree. The sun was low, casting long, thin shadows. I opened it.

Yours, Silas

I held the paper, feeling its weight. A new adventure, a new challenge, shimmered on the periphery. Not a threat, not like before. A choice. A chance to step back into that world, not as a fugitive or a resource, but as a voice. A guardian.

I looked out at my garden, at the path, at the quiet house. The frost wasn’t coming tonight, but a different kind of weather was. A decision. I felt no panic, only a calm readiness. The river had found its bed; it could handle a new tributary. I would write back. I would tell him to support it, with the strictest protocols. It was not an ending. It was a new form of tending.

Scene 4

That evening, after a simple supper, I found myself drawn not to the window, but to the bathroom. The room had changed. The avocado green beast was gone, replaced by a plain white tub. The mildew smell was conquered by plain soap and the faint, clean scent of bleach. But it was still the room.

The seeking had been a spiral, not a line. It had brought me back here, to a tub of water, to a body. But it was a different here. A found here.

I had sought God in visions, in artifacts, in ecstatic dissolution. I got out, the cool air raising goosebumps on my skin. I toweled dry, pulled on soft, worn clothes, and walked through the quiet house to the back door. I stepped onto the porch. The sky was fully dark now, a vast expanse salted with stars. The garden was a pool of deeper shadow, sleeping.

The door was open. I was home. I had learned the weather of my keeping. It was not always calm, but I was anchored. The internal conflict had not been slain; it had been composted. It fed the soil from which my peace grew.

The last time I saw God, I was forty-seven years old, sitting on my own porch in the dark, feeling the immense, tender specificity of it all—the chipped paint on the step, the scent of woodsmoke from a neighbor’s chimney, the memory of a mother’s perfume, the future of a daffodil bulb sleeping in the earth—and knowing, with a certainty that required no vision at all, that I was a part of it. The first time I saw a ghost, I was forty-eight years old and standing in my own kitchen, washing a single coffee mug under water so hot it turned my skin the color of a summer rose. The house was quiet, the kind of quiet that follows a long, good exhaustion. The porch light was off, the garden a sleeping rumor beyond the glass. I was thinking of nothing in particular—the way the suds clung to the ceramic, the faint, soapy smell of lemon, the pleasant ache in my lower back from turning the compost pile that afternoon. I rinsed the mug, set it on the wire rack to drip-dry. I reached to turn off the faucet.

The spiritual seeking had turned a corner. It was no longer about looking for a presence. It was about acknowledging an absence. I was nine years old. I was in the avocado green tub. One foot was propped on the cold lip, my knee a sharp, bony island. The radio played Diana Ross through static. A red bead of blood welled on my shin.

The shock was total. It was not a memory. It was the present. The vivid, aching, terrifying present. The project of becoming.

I stared at the blood. The tiny planet of pain.

The pressure change in the air happened again. The bathroom took a deep, silent breath. The air went thick, honeyed.

I looked up.

God was there. Not as a voice. Not as a light. As a quality of the air in the corner of the room, by the wicker hamper. A concentrated patch of attention. A listening.

I knew it. I knew it was the listening that had just written a universe. I knew it was what I would become. I knew this moment was the hook, the flaw, the corrupt file that would forever stall the perfect silence.

We regarded each other. The child with the razor. The future listening that the child would, through a long and painful journey of seeking, eventually become.

I understood everything. The seeker in me understood the seeker’s end. The child in me felt the awe and the terror.

The listening—God, myself, the completed seeker—did not speak. It had no need. Its presence was a communication. It said: This is the cut. This is the first word of the poem. It is also the last. The poem is circular. You are both the writer and the written.

The knowledge flooded me, a nine-year-old consciousness not built to hold it. It was a crushing, glorious weight. I saw the 24 chapters of my life not as a line, but as a ring. I saw the woman in the white room, unraveling. I saw the mother’s face. I heard my own final instruction: Remember me listening.

And I knew my task. Not the future seeker’s task. My task, here, now, in the bathroom.

I had to remember.

I had to remember this moment wrong. Not as a moment I saw God. But as a moment I cut myself. A moment of small, domestic pain and shame. I had to forget the vast listening in the corner. I had to misinterpret the pressure change as the house settling. I had to bury the knowledge so deep it would become an instinct, a seeking, a lifelong pull toward a static I could not name.

I had to start the journey. I had to become the seeker who would one day become the listening.

It was the only way to close the circle.

Tears filled my eyes. Not from the sting of the cut. From the enormity of the choice. To know the destination, and to choose the road. To have spoken the universe into being, and to now become the child who must discover it, word by painful word.

I looked at the listening in the corner. I gave a tiny, almost imperceptible nod.

The quality of attention shifted. It softened. It became… maternal. It was the off-key song. It was the fevered embrace. It was the love that was also a cage, the comfort that was also a burden.

It was proud of me.

Then, it faded. Not vanishing, but dissolving back into the fabric of the bathroom. The air lost its honeyed thickness. The smell of mildew and Jean Naté returned, ordinary and overwhelming.

I was alone.

I looked down at my shin. The red bead of blood trembled, then traced a thin, perfect path toward my ankle.

I heard my mother’s voice through the door, frayed with a worry I could not yet name. “Everything okay in there?”

I took a shuddering breath. I let the knowledge drain away, like water down a drain. I let the awe become confusion. I let the recognition become a child’s strange fancy. I buried the hook deep, where it could fester and grow and pull me through a lifetime.

I made my voice small. I made it mine.

“Fine!” I called back.

And I reached for a towel to wipe away the blood, beginning the long, beautiful, terrible project of forgetting, so that I might someday remember.
